Part 01

> New Starter — Setting Up Claude for the First Time

A complete, hand-holding walkthrough for setting up Claude properly. Designed for someone who's never used Claude before but has probably been using ChatGPT, Gemini or similar. Follow the steps in order. Takes about 30 to 40 minutes. You only do this once.

What you'll end up with at the end of this section
  • Claude Desktop installed, signed in, and configured
  • A CLAUDE.md file that tells Claude who you are and how you work (its permanent instructions)
  • A Context.md file for the stuff that changes (current goals, targets, focus areas)
  • Any genuinely useful info from your old AI (ChatGPT, Gemini etc.) brought across
  • Memory turned on so Claude remembers you between chats
Before you start, have these ready
  • 30 to 40 minutes (you don't want to pause halfway through)
  • Your existing AI tool open in another tab (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude.ai, Microsoft Copilot, etc.)
  • Your work email and sign-in details
  • A text editor — TextEdit on Mac or Notepad on Windows. Both come free with your computer.

Step 1 — Download Claude Desktop and sign in

#What To Do
1Open your web browser and go to claude.ai/download
2Click the button for your computer (Mac or Windows). The app will download.
3Install the app the same way you would any other — double-click the file, follow the prompts.
4Open Claude Desktop. Sign in with your work email.
5If asked about a plan, ask whoever runs IT at your business. Claude has a free version but you'll usually want Pro or Team for proper work.

Once you're signed in and looking at the home screen of Claude Desktop, you're ready for Step 2. Leave the app open in the background.

Step 2 — Decide which existing AI to mine

You've probably been using another AI before this — ChatGPT, Gemini, or one of the others. We're going to use it to do most of the heavy lifting. It already knows things about you, and we can pull that knowledge into Claude rather than starting from scratch.

Pick the AI you've had the most conversations with. That's the one with the most useful memory of you.

Never used an AI before?

That's fine. Skip straight to Step 6 — you won't have anything to mine, but the interview prompt still works, you'll just need to be a bit more thorough with your answers.

Step 3 — Turn Memory on in that AI

Memory is what makes the whole exercise work. Without it, your AI has nothing to share about you. Here's how to enable it on each of the main ones — find yours below.

If You're UsingHow To Turn Memory On
ChatGPTClick your profile (bottom-left or top-right) → Settings → Personalisation → toggle Memory on.
GeminiClick your profile (top-right) → Settings → Personalisation. Turn on Personalisation and check Saved Info is enabled in your Google account.
Claude.aiSettings → Profile → toggle Memory on. (Yes, you're using Claude to set up Claude. It works.)

Step 4 — Find the gold in your chat history

Most old chats are throwaway — one-off questions, things you asked once and never came back to. But some have genuine value: frameworks you built, decisions you made, voice docs, ongoing strategy notes. This prompt asks your AI to find them.

Paste this into a fresh chat in your existing AI.

Find What's Worth Bringing Across

paste into your AI
I'm moving from you to a different AI assistant. Before I do, help me work out which of our past conversations are actually worth bringing across vs leaving behind. Most of our history is probably throwaway. I want the gold.

Go through everything you remember from our chat history and saved memory, and produce a triaged list using three categories:

## KEEP — Worth bringing across in full
Chats or threads that contain reference material I'll keep using:
- Frameworks or templates we built together
- Brand voice docs, style guides, tone work
- Strategy docs or plans I keep coming back to
- Decision logs or rationale for important calls
- Reusable prompts or workflows we developed
- Anything I've explicitly called useful

For each, give me: a short title, 1-2 lines on what it covers, why it's worth keeping, and a rough date.

## SUMMARISE — Capture the lesson, leave the chat
Things that aren't worth bringing in full but have a useful pattern, preference or lesson inside them:
- Times I corrected you on style or approach
- Recurring pet peeves I've expressed
- Tools or methods I've said I prefer or avoid
- Working-style insights about me

For each, give me: the lesson in one line, and where it came from roughly.

## BIN — Not worth bringing
Don't list these individually. Just tell me roughly how many chats fall in this pile and what kind of stuff is in it.

RULES:
1. Be honest. If only 3 things are worth keeping, say 3. Don't pad the KEEP list.
2. British English throughout.
3. Don't make up chats you don't remember — if your memory is thin, say so.
4. At the end, give me a recommended action plan: what to do with each KEEP item.

Output as a single markdown document I can save.

Step 5 — Bring across the keepers

Look at the KEEP list your AI produced. For each item, open the original chat, copy the useful content, and save it somewhere. Couple of options:

  • Easiest: save each KEEP item as a plain text file in a folder on your Mac called something like “Claude reference”.
  • Tidier: later in this guide we'll create a Project in Claude — you can drop these into the Project Knowledge so Claude can read them whenever you're inside that Project.
Don't overthink it

If the KEEP list is long, just bring across the top 5 or so. You can always go back for more later. The aim is to capture the actually-useful stuff, not to archive everything.

Step 6 — Generate your CLAUDE.md (the big one)

This is the main event. Paste the prompt below into the same chat you've been using. The AI will interview you and produce a full CLAUDE.md file at the end.

Important: edit the "Who I am" section at the top of the prompt before you paste, so it matches you. Then answer each question one at a time — be specific. Vague answers in, vague file out.

Interview Me and Write My CLAUDE.md

paste into your AI
I'd like you to create me a CLAUDE.md file. Before you write it, I want you to interview me to fill in gaps that you can't answer from memory alone.

WHO I AM (edit this section so it matches you, then keep the rest as-is):
- My name is [your name]
- I work at [company name], a [size] [industry] company
- My role is [your job title]
- My background is [a sentence about your experience]
- I am / am not a developer
- Use British English spelling throughout

WHAT A CLAUDE.md IS:
CLAUDE.md is a plain-text instruction file Claude reads automatically every time I start a chat or open a project. It tells Claude who I am, how I like to be spoken to, my preferences, standards, no-gos, and anything else that would normally take 20 minutes to re-explain. Think of it as an employee handbook for an AI assistant.

HOW THIS WORKS — TWO STEPS:

STEP 1 — INTERVIEW ME
Ask the questions below one at a time. Don't dump them all at once. Wait for my answer before moving on. Ask short follow-ups if my answer is vague.

A. Role and responsibilities
   - What does a typical week look like for me?
   - What am I actually responsible for, beyond the job title?
   - How big is my team, and who reports to me?
   - What's on my plate that I wish wasn't, and what do I love doing most?

B. Who I work with
   - What kind of clients or customers do we serve?
   - Any names, accounts, or types I should treat as sensitive?
   - Key colleagues or stakeholders I mention often?

C. Tools I use day-to-day
   - Main software, platforms, or systems?
   - Reporting tools? Project management? Comms?
   - Anything I rely on that's niche to my role?

D. How I like to work
   - Detail or short summaries?
   - Bullets or prose?
   - More of a numbers person or strategy person?
   - When I ask for “a quick look”, what does that mean to me?

E. Communication style
   - How formal or informal am I in writing?
   - Any phrases I always use, or hate?
   - How do I sign off emails?
   - Anything Claude should never say?

F. Pet peeves and no-gos
   - What annoys me when AI assistants help me?
   - Anything I've been burned by before that Claude should avoid?
   - What should I never paste in for confidentiality?

G. Current priorities
   - What's my biggest focus this quarter?
   - What would make Claude genuinely useful vs just a novelty?

Ask follow-ups if useful. Dig deeper on interesting answers.

STEP 2 — WRITE THE FILE
Once the interview is done AND you've mined memory for anything else useful, write me the CLAUDE.md using these headings (drop any that don't apply):

## About Me
## Communication Style
## How I Work
## My Role and Responsibilities
## My Team and Key People
## Tools I Use
## Client and Account Context
## Project Standards
## Rules and No-Gos
## Writing Style
## Current Priorities

RULES FOR THE FINAL OUTPUT:
1. British English throughout.
2. Plain English. No jargon unless I use it.
3. Specific not generic. “Likes concise” is rubbish; “Wants a 3-bullet summary at the top, then detail below if needed” is useful.
4. Pull paraphrases from my answers where they capture my voice.
5. Don't make things up. If you don't know, leave it out.
6. Output the whole CLAUDE.md as a single markdown code block so I can copy it straight to a file.
7. After the code block, give me a short list of things to review or expand manually.

Start with Step 1 now — just the first question, then wait for my answer.

When the AI gives you the final CLAUDE.md (it'll be in a grey code box), copy everything in that code box. Then in TextEdit or Notepad, paste it in and save the file as CLAUDE.md — somewhere easy to find like your Desktop.

Make sure it's a plain text file

On Mac TextEdit: Format → Make Plain Text before saving. On Windows Notepad it's already plain text. If it's saved as Rich Text or .docx, Claude won't read it properly.

Step 7 — Split it into CLAUDE.md and Context.md

Your CLAUDE.md now has a mix of permanent stuff (your role, your style, your standards) and changing stuff (this quarter's targets, current focus, active accounts). We want those in two separate files so you only edit the changing stuff when something changes — not your whole profile.

Paste this into the same chat, right after it gave you the CLAUDE.md.

Split My File into CLAUDE.md and Context.md

follow-up prompt
Take the CLAUDE.md you just wrote me and split it into two separate documents:

1. CLAUDE.md — only the permanent, stable stuff:
   - Who I am, role, team
   - How I communicate and how I like to be spoken to
   - My working style and preferences
   - My standards and rules
   - Pet peeves and no-gos
   - Tools I use day-to-day (these are stable)
   - Key people I work with regularly

2. Context.md — anything that will change within 3 to 6 months:
   - Current quarter's priorities and goals
   - Specific revenue or performance targets
   - Current accounts or projects I'm focused on
   - Active initiatives
   - Anything time-bound or KPI-bound

Output both as separate markdown code blocks so I can save them as two separate files. After the code blocks, give me a one-line rule of thumb for deciding where new info should go in future.

Save the second file as Context.md alongside your CLAUDE.md.

The rule of thumb going forward

If it'll still be true in 6 months, it goes in CLAUDE.md. If it might change before then, it goes in Context.md.

Step 8 — Paste your CLAUDE.md into Claude Desktop

This is where Claude actually starts using your file.

#What To Do
1Open Claude Desktop.
2Click your name in the bottom-left corner.
3Click Settings.
4Click Profile on the left.
5Scroll to the box labelled “What personal preferences should Claude consider in responses?”
6Open your CLAUDE.md file (the one you saved in Step 6). Click inside it, select all (Cmd+A on Mac, Ctrl+A on Windows), copy (Cmd+C / Ctrl+C).
7Click into the Claude box and paste (Cmd+V / Ctrl+V).
8Click Save.
9While you're here: toggle Memory on. It's a switch on the same page.

Claude now reads your profile at the start of every chat. You don't have to re-explain yourself ever again.

Step 9 — Set up a Project for your Context.md

Your Context.md goes somewhere different. Projects in Claude are like folders for related chats — you can give each Project its own knowledge base. Drop your Context.md in there, plus any KEEP items from Step 5, and Claude reads it all whenever you're working inside that Project.

#What To Do
1In Claude Desktop, click Projects in the left sidebar.
2Click New Project.
3Name it something like “My Current Context” or “[your role] — This Quarter”.
4Find the Project Knowledge section (or “Add to knowledge” button).
5Open your Context.md file, select all, copy, paste it into the Project Knowledge box. Save.
6Optional: add any KEEP items from Step 5 to the same Project Knowledge.

Step 10 — Quick test it's working

Final check.

  1. Start a brand new chat in Claude Desktop (not inside a Project — the main chat).
  2. Type: “What do you know about me and how I like to work?”
  3. Press send.

If Claude plays back your role, your style and your preferences accurately — you're done. Set up complete.

If Claude is vague or generic — your CLAUDE.md needs more detail. Go back to your AI and either redo the interview with more specific answers, or ask it to expand the file.

What now?

You've done the heavy lifting. Claude knows who you are, you don't have to repeat yourself, and the useful bits of your old AI history are in the right places.

Memory deep dive

Memory is the bit most people are confused or nervous about. Here's what you need to know.

What it actually does

When you turn Memory on, Claude saves bits of information about you across chats. So if you say “I prefer short bullet-point answers” in one chat, the next chat already knows. It builds up over time, naturally, from things you tell it.

How it's different from your CLAUDE.md

CLAUDE.mdMemory
Who writes itYou do (or your AI does, via Part 01)Claude does, based on chat
How it's editedYou paste it into SettingsYou can view, edit, or delete entries in Settings
How big it should beA few hundred lines, structuredShort individual entries that build up
Best forPermanent, structured profileBits Claude picks up incidentally

Most people have CLAUDE.md doing 80% of the work and Memory filling in 20% — the casual things you mention in passing.

How to view and manage what Claude remembers

  1. Open Claude Desktop.
  2. Click your name (bottom-left) → Settings.
  3. Click Profile.
  4. Find the Memory section. You'll see a list of everything Claude has saved.
  5. Click the bin icon next to any entry to delete it.
  6. To wipe everything, click Clear all memories.

How to add or update a memory mid-chat

Just say it. Examples:

  • “Remember that I always sign off emails with Cheers.”
  • “Forget what I said earlier about preferring long answers — I want short ones from now on.”
  • “Don't remember this conversation.” (tells Claude not to save anything from this chat)

Common questions

Does Memory share between chats and Projects? ▼

Yes. Memory is account-wide. Anything Claude remembers applies to every chat, in every Project, on every device you're signed in on.

Can a colleague see my memories? ▼

No. Memory is personal to your account. Even on a Team plan, your colleagues have their own Memory.

Will Memory remember confidential info I've shared? ▼

Potentially. If you paste in a client's revenue figures and Claude decides that's memorable, it might save a note. Check your Memory list every now and then. If you see something sensitive, delete it.

Should I turn it off then? ▼

For most people, no — the benefit (Claude knowing you) outweighs the risk. But if you regularly handle highly sensitive material, you can either disable Memory or remember to say “don't remember this conversation” at the start of sensitive chats.

Why does Claude sometimes “forget” what I told it? ▼

A few reasons: (1) you didn't say “remember this” and Claude judged it not memorable, (2) Memory has a soft limit and older entries can roll off, (3) the entry is there but it didn't surface in this chat. Worth checking your Memory list to see what's actually saved.

Rule of thumb for Memory

Anything you'd want to put in writing in your CLAUDE.md, put it there. Memory is best for the small incidental things Claude notices about you over time. Don't rely on Memory for anything important — if you'd be annoyed when it forgot, that thing belongs in CLAUDE.md.

Three types of add-ons

Repos = upgrade packs that make Claude smarter overall. Install in Terminal.
Skills = specialist playbooks for specific tasks (SEO, ads, security, etc.). Install in Terminal.
MCP Servers = connectors that plug Claude into apps you already use (Slack, Xero, etc.). Install in Claude Code.

terminal

Run this once per repo/skill in Terminal. Works across every project on your computer.

Part 09

> Where Things Live (and Who Sees Them)

Two different things to understand: where your settings apply (who can see them and when), and where files actually sit on your computer. The first one matters for everyone. The second one only matters if you're using Claude Code with project folders.

Where settings apply — the three levels

This is the bit that confuses most people. When you set something up in Claude, it lives at one of three levels — and which level you pick controls who sees it.

Organisation

Organisation / Team

Set up by your admin (or IT person). Applied to everyone in your company automatically.

Examples:

  • Connectors your admin pushes out to the whole team (e.g. shared Trello, Monday)
  • Skills enabled at organisation level in Cowork
  • Team plan-wide settings managed via the Anthropic admin console

You'd notice this as: things that appear already enabled when you log in, that you didn't set up yourself.

Personal

Personal / Account

Only on your account. Colleagues don't see them, even if you're on the same Team plan.

Examples:

  • Your CLAUDE.md pasted in Settings → Profile
  • Connectors you add yourself in Settings → Connectors
  • Skills you toggle on in Settings → Customize → Skills
  • Memory toggled on for your account

This is the level you'll use most of the time.

Project

Project

Only applies inside that one Project in Claude Desktop. The moment you start a chat outside it, the rules don't apply.

Examples:

  • Your Context.md pasted into Project Knowledge
  • Project-specific instructions on top of your CLAUDE.md
  • Reference files you uploaded just for that Project
  • Any KEEP items you brought across from your old AI

Use this for: anything that's specific to one piece of work and shouldn't bleed into other chats.

Quick lookup — what goes where?

ThingLevelWhere You Set It

Rules of thumb
  • If you'd say “I want this for me, not the team” → Personal
  • If you'd say “I want this for one specific job or client” → Project
  • If you'd say “I want everyone in the business to have this” → Organisation (and you'll need admin access or to ask your IT person)
Why this matters

A common confusion: you install something and a colleague can't find it. Or you can't find something a colleague set up. Almost always, it's because that thing was set at a Personal level on one of your accounts — not pushed to the whole organisation.

The other common one: people set things at Personal level when they really want it at Project level, then their instructions for one client bleed into chats about a completely different client. Project level keeps things tidy.

Where files actually live on disk

Only relevant if you're using Claude Code with project folders. If you're mainly using Chat and Cowork, you can skip this bit — everything you need to know is above.

Global settings and skills (shared across all your Code projects)

~/.claude/
  ├── skills/                 <-- All installed add-ons
  │   ├── superpowers/     <-- Example add-on
  │   └── ...
  └── projects/
      └── [project-name]/
          └── memory/      <-- Claude's memory for this project

The ~ symbol means your home folder — on Mac that's /Users/yourname/.

Your project folder (one per Code project)

~/Sites/my-project/     <-- Your project folder
  ├── CLAUDE.md         <-- House rules for THIS project
  ├── Context.md       <-- Background info
  └── [your files...]
Heads up — Desktop and Code use different homes

Your Desktop CLAUDE.md (the one in Settings → Profile) is separate from the file-based CLAUDE.md that lives in a Code project folder. They're both called CLAUDE.md but they apply differently:

  • Desktop / Settings CLAUDE.md → reads on every chat across the whole app
  • Project folder CLAUDE.md → only reads when Code is opened against that folder

For most people, you'll only ever set the first one (in Settings). Only developers using Code on a per-project basis need the second.

Start Every New Project With This

The single most important prompt in this guide. Paste it as the very first thing you say in a new project.

The Recommended First Message

new project
Hi Claude. I've just created this project folder and I need your help setting it up properly and building it out.

Before we do anything, here's what you need to know about me:
- I'm a business decision maker, not a developer
- Always talk to me in plain, non-technical language
- When you make changes, explain what you did and why in terms I can understand
- Don't build anything until we've talked through what I need first
- Ask me questions before making assumptions

Let's start by getting this project set up:

1. Ask me about the project — what it is, who it's for, what I want it to achieve, and any preferences I have. Use these answers to create the CLAUDE.md and Context.md files.

2. Set up your memory system for this project so you remember our conversations.

3. Check that global skills (like Superpowers) are installed and available.

4. Once setup is done, give me a status report and then ask me what I'd like to work on first.

Let's go.

Health Check

any project
Check if this project has the following configuration files set up properly. For each one, tell me: does it exist, where is it, and is it empty or does it have content? Give me a simple status report.

1. CLAUDE.md (or AGENTS.md) — the "house rules" file in the project root
2. Context.md — background info file in the project root
3. Memory system — look for ~/.claude/projects/ folder that matches this project, and check for MEMORY.md inside it
4. Skills — check if ~/.claude/skills/ exists and what's installed there

For anything that's missing or empty, tell me what it should contain in plain, non-technical language. I'm not a developer — I'm a business decision maker.

Format your answer as a simple table:
| File | Status | What to Do |

Get Up to Speed

start of session
Read the CLAUDE.md, Context.md, and your memory for this project. Then give me a brief summary of:
1. What this project is
2. Where we left off
3. What's pending or next

Keep it to a few sentences. I'll tell you what I want to work on today.

End of Session

wrapping up
Before we wrap up, please do the following:

1. Update CLAUDE.md — Add any new rules, preferences, or decisions we made during this session that should apply going forward. Don't remove existing content, just add what's new.

2. Update Context.md — Add any new context about the project, business, audience, or strategy that came up during this session.

3. Update Memory — Save anything important about me, my preferences, or project status that you should remember for next time.

4. Give me a short summary of what was added to each file so I know what changed.

Talk to me in plain language — I'm not a developer.

End of Day Debrief

full day wrap-up
End of day debrief. Please do the following:

1. Review everything we worked on today and summarise the key decisions, changes, and progress made.
2. Update CLAUDE.md with any new project rules or preferences discovered today.
3. Update Context.md with any new business context, goals, or strategic decisions from today.
4. Update Memory with anything you should remember about me, the project, or our working style for future sessions.
5. Create a brief "session log" summary I can refer back to — what we did, what's done, and what's still pending.

Keep everything in plain language. I'm a business decision maker, not a developer.

Remember This

any time
Remember this for all future sessions: [write what you want remembered here]

Explain What Just Happened

when confused
Explain what you just did in plain, non-technical language. I'm not a developer. Tell me:
1. What changed?
2. Why did you do it?
3. What does it mean for the project?
4. Is there anything I need to decide or approve?

Weekly Status Report

once a week
Give me a weekly project status report in plain language:

1. What has been built or changed this week?
2. What's working well?
3. What still needs to be done?
4. Are there any decisions I need to make?
5. Update the Context.md and CLAUDE.md if anything is outdated.

Format it like a business report, not a technical changelog. I'm a decision maker, not a developer.
